Section 1 · Foundations

What is Didactics?

🏫
The Teaching Exam Question You Must Get Right
Every teaching exam in Morocco tests whether you understand what Didactics actually means. Many candidates confuse it with Pedagogy or Psychology. By the end of this lesson, you will never make that mistake again.

The word Didactics comes from the Greek didaktikos, meaning "skilled at teaching." It is a branch of Educational Sciences that sits at the intersection of three elements: the teacher, the learner, and knowledge. It asks: how do these three interact most effectively?

The Official Definition

Didactics = The science and art of teaching
It focuses on: How teachers, students, and knowledge interact
It is the theory and practice of instruction — the WHY and HOW of teaching.

Notice that Didactics is both a science (systematic, evidence-based) and an art (creative, adaptive). This dual nature is key to understanding why it draws from both psychology and education.

🔎Didactics vs. Pedagogy: Pedagogy focuses on the general process of how education is delivered. Didactics zooms in on specific subject teaching — the content, the method, and the learner together. Both claim Didactics is part of them, making it a bridge between theory and practice.
🧠Why does it matter for your exam? The teaching exam tests whether you can be a thinking teacher, not just a doing one. Knowing Didactics means you can justify WHY you choose a certain method, adapt it to your learners, and reflect on the outcome.
🔑Key connections: Didactics draws from Psychology (how students learn), Linguistics (the nature of language), and Pedagogy (the art of teaching). It connects all of these into practical classroom decisions.
Section 2 · Core Vocabulary

The 5 Key Terms — Tap to Reveal

These five terms are the most tested concepts in any teaching exam. They are often confused. Tap each card to reveal the full definition and an example. Say the definition aloud before flipping!

💡Memory tip: Think of them as levels of a pyramid — from the broadest philosophy (Approach) down to the smallest classroom step (Procedure). Each level depends on the one above it.
🌍
Approach
Tap to reveal definition
A set of beliefs or theories about how language is learned. It is the broad philosophy or framework — the WHY behind teaching decisions.

Example: Behaviourism → language is learned through repetition and reinforcement.

"I believe language is best learned through real communication" = Communicative Approach.
Broadest Level
📋
Method
Tap to reveal definition
The practical application of an approach. A set of specific techniques and procedures used to teach based on a chosen approach.

Example: Audio-Lingual Method (based on Behaviourism) → uses drills, repetition, and habit formation.

"I will use drills and dialogues to build habits" = A Method decision.
Applied Level
🎯
Technique
Tap to reveal definition
A specific classroom activity or task. The most practical and visible part of teaching — what learners actually do.

Examples:
• PPP (Presentation, Practice, Production)
• Mind Maps
• Jigsaw Reading
• Brainstorming
• Role-play
Activity Level
🧩
Methodology
Tap to reveal definition
A teacher's personal way of choosing and combining techniques. It is flexible and depends on the classroom context, learners' needs, and the teacher's own style.

Example: Using group work from CLT + drills from ALM + music from Suggestopedia.

Methodology = the teacher's unique teaching fingerprint.
Personal Level
📝
Procedure
Tap to reveal definition
The step-by-step instructions for applying a method in a specific lesson. It includes sequence of activities, timing, and classroom management.

Example:
Step 1: Present the dialogue (3 min)
Step 2: Practice in pairs (5 min)
Step 3: Role-play freely (7 min)
Classroom Level
Section 3 · Critical Distinction

Theory of Language vs Theory of Learning

This distinction is extremely common in teaching exams and often confuses candidates. These two theories form the foundation of every teaching method — and knowing the difference is essential.

💡The quick test: Ask yourself — "Is this about what language IS (linguistics), or about how people LEARN it (psychology)?" If it's about grammar, structure, and meaning → Theory of Language. If it's about memory, habits, or cognition → Theory of Learning.

Theory of Language (Linguistics)

What language IS: its structure, grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation
How language FUNCTIONS: communication, interaction, social use
CLT Example: Language is a tool for real-life communication (not just grammar rules)
GTM Example: Language is a set of structural rules to be analyzed and translated

Theory of Learning (Psychology)

How people LEARN: through behavior, thinking, memory, or social interaction
What processes help learners ACQUIRE language
Behaviourism Example: Learning occurs through repetition and reinforcement
Cognitivism Example: Learning occurs through mental processing and understanding
MethodTheory of LanguageTheory of Learning
Grammar Translation (GTM)Language = structure + literary textMemorization + mental discipline
Audio-Lingual Method (ALM)Language = patterns + habitsBehaviourism — repetition + reinforcement
Communicative LT (CLT)Language = communication toolSocial interaction + authentic use
Silent WayLanguage = rule system to discoverCognitivism — self-discovery + problem solving
Total Physical Response (TPR)Language = imperative commandsBehaviourism + comprehension first
🧠Exam insight: When an exam question asks "What are the theoretical foundations of the Audio-Lingual Method?", you should mention BOTH: (1) Structuralism as the theory of language, and (2) Behaviourism as the theory of learning.

The Teaching Pyramid — How the Terms Connect

The five key terms form a hierarchy — from the broadest, most abstract level (Approach) down to the most specific classroom step (Procedure). Each level determines the one below it.

Broad → Specific
🌍 APPROACH
Philosophy / Beliefs
↓
📋 METHOD
Practical System
↓
📝 PROCEDURE
Step-by-step Plan
↓
🎯 TECHNIQUE
Classroom Activity
🧩 METHODOLOGY = Teacher's personal mix of all the above
💡A real example top to bottom:
Approach: I believe language is learned through natural communication (Communicative Approach).
Method: I will use CLT — role-plays, information gaps, authentic tasks.
Procedure: Step 1 (5 min): warm-up question. Step 2 (10 min): read authentic dialogue. Step 3 (15 min): role-play task.
Technique: Information-gap activity where students exchange missing facts.
Methodology: I sometimes add grammar correction from ALM when students repeat an error.

A Teacher Describes Her Approach
~30 seconds · British English · browser audio

Teacher: "I believe that language is best learned when students actually use it to communicate — that's my core belief, my philosophy. Based on that, I've chosen to teach using Communicative Language Teaching as my main system. In today's lesson, my plan is to start with a five-minute warm-up question, then have students read an authentic email, and finally do a role-play in pairs — those are my steps for the lesson. For the role-play itself, students are missing pieces of information that only their partner has — that's the specific activity I've designed. And sometimes I'll borrow a pronunciation drill from the Audio-Lingual Method when I hear a repeated error — that's my own personal way of mixing things up."

Section 6 · Critical Thinking Task

Apply What You Know — Scenario Analysis

Each scenario below describes a teaching decision. Identify the term being used and explain your reasoning. Say your answer aloud before reading the model answer!

🗣️
Task 1 — Oral Analysis (2 minutes)
For each scenario, say: "This is a [term] because…" Explain WHY using the definitions you learned. Push yourself to justify your answer fully.
Scenario A: "I pair students together. Student A has a train timetable with gaps; Student B has the missing times. They must ask questions to complete their tables." → What term?

Scenario B: "I teach English using the idea that students learn best by discovering rules themselves — I rarely explain grammar directly." → What term?

Scenario C: "My lesson today: 8 min grammar presentation → 12 min controlled practice → 15 min free writing. Students finish with a creative paragraph." → What term?

Scenario D: "I use CLT in general, but I'll throw in a quick Skinner-style drill when students keep making the same pronunciation error, and I use background music occasionally from Suggestopedia." → What term?
✅Model answers:
A → Technique (information-gap activity — a specific classroom task)
B → Approach (the belief/philosophy that inductive discovery is best)
C → Procedure (the sequenced steps and timing of a lesson)
D → Methodology (personal combination of multiple methods and techniques)
💬
Task 2 — Discussion Question
Think about this question carefully. Discuss it with a study partner, or write your answer in the box below:
Can a teacher use multiple Approaches at the same time?

Consider: A teacher believes in both communicative interaction (CLT) AND habit formation (Behaviourism). Is this possible? What would this be called? How would you justify this in your teaching philosophy statement?
🧠Hint for the exam: Yes — a teacher can hold multiple belief systems. When a teacher consciously combines beliefs and methods based on context and learner needs, this is called the Eclectic Approach — the most commonly practiced approach in real classrooms. Your personal Methodology reflects this flexibility.
Section 7 · Written Production

Write Your Teaching Philosophy

This type of task appears frequently in teaching exams. Write a short paragraph (60–80 words) describing your personal teaching approach — using at least 4 of the 5 key terms correctly. Examiners look for: accurate use of terms, clear reasoning, and practical examples.

✍️Structure your paragraph:
1. State your core belief about language learning (→ Approach)
2. Name the main method you use (→ Method)
3. Describe your typical lesson steps (→ Procedure)
4. Give a specific classroom activity (→ Technique)
5. Mention how you adapt when needed (→ Methodology)
