The Trap ⚠

The Old with the New

Most teachers use technology just to replace what they already do — like writing on a screen instead of a board. That's not transformation, that's decoration.

The SAMR Model

A 4-stage ladder that shows how deeply technology is transforming a classroom task.

Think of SAMR like renovating a house. You can repaint the walls (Substitution), add new fixtures (Augmentation), knock down walls to redesign rooms (Modification), or tear down the whole thing and build something entirely new (Redefinition). Same house, totally different results.

R

Redefinition Transformation

Technology enables entirely new tasks — things that were impossible before. Students publish to global audiences, collaborate in real time across continents.

TRANS
FORM
M

Modification Transformation

Technology significantly redesigns the task. A quiz becomes a live adaptive system that changes the lesson based on student answers.

TRANS
FORM
A

Augmentation Enhancement

Technology replaces the old tool AND adds useful features. A word processor with spellcheck, hyperlinks, and auto-formatting.

ENHANCE
S

Substitution Enhancement

Technology replaces a non-digital tool with zero functional change. Typing an essay instead of handwriting it — same task, new medium.

ENHANCE
💡 Simple rule: S & A = Enhancement (doing the same thing better). M & R = Transformation (doing something that wasn't possible before).

Who created it?

🎓

Dr. Ruben Puentedura — Harvard University

Puentedura created SAMR to give teachers a practical framework for reflecting on their use of technology. He was influenced by research on how transformative technologies change learning environments. The key insight: technology alone changes nothing — how it's used is everything.

SAMR Deep Dive

Understand each stage fully — with real analogies and examples.

S — Substitution

Direct replacement, no functional change

You swap the old tool for a digital one. The task stays exactly the same. Nothing new is learned, nothing new is possible — you're just using a different medium.

1

What changes?

Only the medium — from physical to digital. The activity, the learning objectives, and the student experience remain identical.

2

Everyday analogy

Imagine you always wrote grocery lists on paper. One day you type the list in your phone's Notes app. Same list, same task — just on a screen.

📱 Grocery list on paper → grocery list in a Notes app. You saved no time, learned nothing new, gained nothing extra.
3

Classroom example

Students write an essay in a notebook. The teacher asks them to type it in Microsoft Word instead. Same essay, same structure, same thinking required.

✍️ Handwritten essay → typed essay in Word. This is SAMR Substitution — no functional improvement, just a digital swap.
4

Is it bad?

Not necessarily! It's a starting point. Every teacher begins here. The question SAMR asks is: can we climb higher?

A — Augmentation

Substitute + meaningful functional improvement

You still replace the old tool, but now the digital version does things the old one couldn't. It's the same task fundamentally — but the tools add genuine value.

1

What changes?

The tool gains new abilities — spellcheck, instant formatting, hyperlinks, search, auto-save. These aren't possible with pen and paper.

2

Everyday analogy

You used to drive to the library to look up information. Now you Google it. You're still "looking things up" — but the digital method is dramatically more efficient.

🔍 Library trip → Google search. Same goal, but the tool added speed, breadth, and convenience. That's Augmentation.
3

Classroom example

Students write the same essay, but now in Google Docs. They benefit from spellcheck, grammar suggestions, the ability to embed hyperlinks, and easy sharing with the teacher.

📄 Essay in Google Docs with spellcheck + embedded sources + instant teacher feedback via comments. Functionally better!
4

Key distinction from S

In Substitution, removing the technology changes nothing. In Augmentation, removing it would make the task noticeably harder or worse.

M — Modification

Technology allows significant task redesign

This is where we cross into Transformation. The task itself is redesigned — not just the tools. The way learning happens fundamentally changes.

1

What changes?

The task itself is reconfigured. Technology doesn't just support the old task — it enables a fundamentally different kind of activity.

2

Everyday analogy

Old way: you fill in a paper feedback form and hand it to a manager. New way: an online form instantly shows aggregated results, highlights patterns, and auto-generates a report.

📊 The task changed. The manager now adapts decisions based on live data — something impossible with paper forms.
3

Classroom example (Socrative)

Instead of a paper quiz, students answer via Socrative on their phones. The teacher sees results in real time, identifies which concept 60% of students got wrong, and immediately adjusts the lesson.

📱 The quiz is no longer just assessment — it becomes a teaching tool that reshapes the lesson as it happens. That's Modification.
4

Why it matters

The teacher's role changes too. Instead of waiting until the end of term to see results, they respond to student understanding minute by minute.

R — Redefinition

Technology creates entirely new, previously impossible tasks

The highest level. Students do things that were literally inconceivable without technology. New kinds of learning, new kinds of outputs, new kinds of audiences.

1

What changes?

Everything. The task didn't exist before technology. You can't even describe it in terms of the old way — there was no "old way."

2

Everyday analogy

Before smartphones, you couldn't share a live video with 10,000 people simultaneously from your bedroom. That task literally did not exist. Live streaming is a Redefinition-level activity.

📡 Live streaming to thousands of viewers is not an "improved" version of anything — it's a brand new kind of communication.
3

Classroom example

Students collaborate live with a class in another country, co-writing a document, video-debating perspectives, and publishing a joint analysis to a public audience who comments in real time.

🌐 No classroom assignment ever worked like this before. The audience is real, the collaboration is global, the product is public. Pure Redefinition.
4

The student's role

At this level, the student is not just completing an assignment — they are a knowledge creator and global participant. The teacher becomes a facilitator and coach.

The Raby Model

Carol Raby (2004) — Maps the teacher's personal journey toward fully integrating ICT into their teaching.

🔑 Key difference from SAMR: SAMR asks "what is technology doing to the task?" Raby asks "where is the teacher in their journey?"

Raby noticed that teachers don't just wake up one day and brilliantly use technology in their teaching. They go through a personal journey — first using it at home, then at work, and finally in the classroom. Her model maps that journey in 3 main contexts, each with sub-stages.

The 3 Contexts — A Stepping Stone Journey

①

Personal Use (Utilisation Personnelle)

The teacher first encounters technology in their private life — at home, with family, out of curiosity. This is where the journey begins.

🏠 A teacher starts using Google Maps instead of paper maps, or WhatsApp instead of phone calls. No teaching involved yet — just personal comfort with technology.
🌱 Sensibilisation

Indirect contact. The teacher hears about technology, sees others use it — but hasn't tried it themselves yet.

🤔 Familiarisation

First attempts. Overcoming fear, mastering basic skills. "I'm learning how this works."

✅ Exploration-Appropriation

Comfortable personal use. Creating documents, searching information, communicating with friends/family.

②

Professional Use (Utilisation Professionnelle)

ICT migrates into the teacher's professional non-teaching life. They use it for administrative work, colleague communication, research — but not yet in the classroom.

🏢 The teacher emails colleagues, creates lesson plans in Word, looks up curriculum documents online, joins a professional WhatsApp group. Still not in the classroom — but getting closer.
📧 Communication

Emailing colleagues, using staff platforms, joining professional groups.

📁 Administration

Creating lesson plans, timetables, and reports digitally.

🔎 Research

Using the internet to find teaching resources, new methods, curriculum updates.

③

Pedagogical Use (Utilisation Pédagogique)

ICT finally enters the classroom. This is the main destination of Raby's model — and it has 3 sub-stages of its own.

🎓 The teacher now uses technology with students, designing activities around digital tools to support and transform learning.
🔭 Exploration

ICT as a lecture aid — the teacher uses a projector, shows videos, supplements traditional teaching.

🌊 Infusion

ICT woven into regular teaching. Students use it to access information, collaborate, and demonstrate understanding.

⭐ Appropriation Pédagogique

The apex. ICT is fully integrated into meaningful, transformative learning experiences. See the next section!

💡 Why does the journey start at home, not at school? ⌄

Raby found that teachers are people first, educators second. They build confidence with technology in low-stakes environments — their homes — before bringing it into the high-stakes environment of the classroom. You don't learn to swim in the deep end. You start in the shallow water.

⚡ What's the biggest insight from Raby? ⌄

Technical fluency ≠ Pedagogical fluency. A teacher who is great at using Instagram has not automatically become great at using technology to teach. These are separately developed competencies. You can be a tech wizard personally and a total beginner pedagogically. The journey through all 3 contexts is necessary.

  • Knowing how to use a tool ≠ Knowing how to teach with that tool
  • The same psychological barriers (fear, insecurity, time) resurface at every new stage
  • This means teacher training must go beyond "here's how the software works"
🔄 Is the journey linear? Can you go backwards? ⌄

No — Raby's model is non-linear. A teacher might be at Appropriation Pédagogique in one area (e.g., using Socrative for assessment) but back at Familiarisation with a brand-new tool (e.g., VR headsets). Every new technology resets the journey to some degree. This is why teachers never fully "arrive" — they are always in motion.

Appropriation Pédagogique ⭐

The highest stage in Raby's model — where ICT is fully and meaningfully integrated into transformative teaching.

"Regular, meaningful ICT activities designed for both knowledge transmission AND knowledge construction."

Think of Appropriation Pédagogique as the moment a driver stops thinking about how to drive and just drives to where they want to go. The teacher is no longer thinking "how do I use this tool?" — they think "how do I design the best learning experience for my students?" The tool is invisible. The learning is everything.

🔄 Feature 1

Transmission AND Construction

The teacher doesn't just transmit knowledge — they design activities where students also construct knowledge. Learning flows in both directions.

🎓 Feature 2

Dual Competency Development

Students develop subject knowledge (e.g., history content) AND transversal skills (e.g., critical thinking, collaboration, digital literacy) — in the same activity.

🌱 Feature 3

Active & Significant Learning

Learning is actif — students are doing things, making decisions, creating outputs. It's significatif — it connects to real purposes and real audiences.

🛠 Feature 4

Teacher as Catalyst

The teacher's role shifts completely: from being a transmitter → to being a facilitator, idea-trigger, and catalyst. They design the experience; students live it.

A concrete example

🌍 Appropriation Pédagogique in action

A history teacher designs a project where students collaborate with a partner class in Germany to build a shared digital archive of World War II personal testimonies.

  • Students research, write, translate, and interview (knowledge construction)
  • They learn about history content AND collaboration, empathy, and digital research skills (dual competencies)
  • The archive is published and used by other schools (authentic, significant purpose)
  • The teacher designed the structure but the students drive the content (teacher as catalyst)
✅ This is Appropriation Pédagogique. Technology is the vehicle. Deep learning is the destination.

ICT Integration Challenges

Why don't all teachers reach Redefinition or Appropriation Pédagogique? Here's what gets in the way.

📉

1. Motivation & Engagement

Teachers struggle to maintain student motivation when technology is used without clear pedagogical purpose. If the tech feels like a gimmick, students disengage faster than in a traditional lesson.

Example: A teacher uses Kahoot every single lesson because "students love it" — but the questions never go beyond basic recall. Fun, but no transformation happening.
🔧

2. Technical Barriers

Unreliable internet, insufficient devices, no tech support, and outdated software make it impossible for teachers to plan confidently around technology. If the WiFi drops during a Socrative quiz, the lesson collapses.

Raby's model explains this: a teacher at the Familiarisation stage gets knocked back to Sensibilisation every time the technology fails unexpectedly.
🎯

3. Pedagogical Misalignment

Using technology for its own sake — "using tech because it's cool" rather than because it serves a specific learning objective. This is the classic SAMR trap: staying permanently at Substitution because it feels "innovative" without actually being so.

SAMR diagnosis: the task is S-level but the teacher thinks it's R-level because a device was involved.
🧑‍🏫

4. Teacher Training Gap

Most teacher training focuses on how to use the software — not how to teach with it pedagogically. This is the core insight of Raby: technical fluency ≠ pedagogical fluency. You can be an expert at using an iPad and still use it only at Substitution level in the classroom.

Solution: Training must shift from "here's how the tool works" to "here's how to design transformative learning experiences with this tool."
📊

5. Measuring ICT Impact

It's genuinely hard to prove that using a specific digital tool improved learning outcomes. Without evidence, school leaders don't invest, and teachers don't feel validated for the extra effort integration requires.

SAMR helps here — if you're at Modification or Redefinition, the impact is structural and observable in the kinds of tasks students produce.
